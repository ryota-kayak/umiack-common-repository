Flowing through the center of Tokyo are the Nihonbashi, Kanda, and Sumida Rivers. We will explore these rivers by kayak, which served as the main arteries of the great city of Edo and still allow you to deeply feel their rich history even in today's modernized Tokyo. The tour lasts up to about 5 hours, adjusted according to your physical strength and the tide conditions.
Launching our kayaks from the Sumida River, we will head into the Nihonbashi River. Look up at the Metropolitan Expressway, built in 1963, which twists over the river like a living creature. Admire the majestic Kirin (mythical winged creature) statues resting on the Nihonbashi Bridge, rebuilt in 1911. Paddling further into the outer moat, we will pass under "Tokiwa Bridge," the oldest stone arch bridge in Tokyo, built in 1877 (making it 149 years old as of 2026). As we reach the outer moat of the Imperial Palace, you will find massive stone walls constructed 400 years ago through the "Tenka Bushin" (public works ordered by the Shogunate). While searching for the engraved crests on these outer moat stones, we will soon arrive at the ruins of the Hitotsubashi Gate. Embark on a time-travel adventure through the heart of Tokyo, where the scenery shifts as if journeying back in time.

Highlights

  1. [Sumida River] The Main Street of Edo

    Known as "Okawa" (Great River) during the Edo period, this was Tokyo's main street, bustling with boats transporting goods. The wide-open sky and sense of freedom you experience looking up from a kayak are exceptional.

  2. [Toyomi Bridge] The Beauty of Massive Iron Structures

    Spanning the entrance to the Nihonbashi River, this Vierendeel truss bridge was completed in 1927. Its unique shape, resembling a ladder laid on its side, makes it a rare and valuable civil engineering heritage in Japan. In fact, this is the very path the 47 Ronin (Ako Roshi) took as they headed toward Sengakuji Temple after their successful raid on Kira's mansion, crossing the Eitai Bridge. Come see the solid, beautiful ironwork that has witnessed turning points in history up close.

  3. [Nihonbashi] The Vital Hub That Supported Edo's Logistics

    Spanning the Nihonbashi River, this bridge served as the logistical heart of Edo and remains the starting point of modern Japan's road network. Completed in 1911, the current double-arch stone bridge features majestic statues of Kirin—mythical creatures granted "wings" as a symbol of wishes for prosperity. Gazing up at these statues from the water is truly a breathtaking sight.

  4. [Tokiwabashi Gate & Tokiwa Bridge] Tokyo's Oldest Stone Arch Bridge

    Spanning the Nihonbashi River, this is the oldest stone arch bridge in Tokyo, completed in 1877 (making it 149 years old as of 2026). It repurposes stones from the "Tokiwabashi Gate," a former gate of Edo Castle, preserving the legacy of Japan's modernization (Civilization and Enlightenment) during the Meiji era. We will paddle our kayaks right through the remnants of this historic gate.

  5. [Imperial Palace Outer Moat & Engraved Stones] Proof of the Shogunate's Public Works by Feudal Lords

    Here lie the stone walls of Edo Castle, completely hidden from those walking on the streets above. These massive stones form the outer fortifications (outer moat) of Edo Castle. If you look closely, you will see "crests" engraved all over the stones—marks left by various feudal lord (Daimyo) families to identify the sections they were responsible for building. Try searching for these marks and feel the spirit of craftsmen from 400 years ago, all from the uniquely close vantage point of a kayak.

  6. [Hitotsubashi Gate Ruins] The Castle Gate Associated with the Hitotsubashi Tokugawa Family

    These are the ruins of a castle gate located near the residence of the Hitotsubashi family, one of the three privileged branch families (Gosankyo) that supported the Tokugawa Shogunate. The overwhelming scale of the massive stone walls viewed from the water speaks to the formidable defenses of the former Edo Castle. The name originates from a single log bridge ("ippon no marukibashi") that crossed this spot before Tokugawa Ieyasu entered the region. Take in this historic site associated with the last shogun, Tokugawa Yoshinobu, from the unique perspective of the water.

  7. [Asakusabashi Boathouses] The Deep Lingering Scent of Edo's Geisha District Culture

    The course may be altered depending on tidal currents.

    Near the mouth of the Kanda River, from Yanagibashi to Asakusabashi, the scenery of traditional boathouses (funayado) still remains. It is an atmospheric area strongly retaining the essence of the boat transport culture that has continued since the Edo period. Yanagibashi, located at the confluence of the Kanda River, was once celebrated alongside Shinbashi as one of the highest-class geisha districts (Hanamachi). The boathouses lining the riverbanks today still breathe the refined culture of Edo boat-leisure, once cherished even by Meiji-era founding fathers like Ito Hirobumi.

  8. [Eitai Bridge] The Steel Arch Known as the Samurai of the Sumida River

    The course may be altered depending on tidal currents.

    A massive steel arch bridge spanning the Sumida River, characterized by its powerful design, earning it the nickname "The Samurai of the Sumida River." It is designated as a National Important Cultural Property.

  9. [Kiyosu Bridge] The Rare Structural Beauty of a Steel Suspension Bridge

    The course may be altered depending on tidal currents.

    A famous bridge spanning the Sumida River, modeled after a suspension bridge in Cologne, Germany. It features an elegant design that has earned it the title "The Lady of the Sumida River." It is designated as a National Important Cultural Property.

  10. [Ryogoku Bridge] A Vital Transportation Hub Intertwined with the History of Fireworks

    The course may be altered depending on tidal currents.

    Spanning the Sumida River, this bridge gets its name from connecting the "two provinces" (Ryogoku) of Musashi and Shimousa. It has flourished as a key transportation point since the Edo period and is deeply tied to the history of Tokyo's summer fireworks.

  11. [Kachidoki Bridge] The Structural Beauty of a Drawbridge That No Longer Opens

    The course may be altered depending on tidal currents.

    Spanning the Sumida River, this is one of the few bascule bridges (drawbridges) in Japan. Although its operations are permanently halted today, its beautiful structure has earned it a designation as a National Important Cultural Property.

  12. [History] Edo's Canals and a Metropolis Time Travel

    Did you know such a place exists right in the center of Tokyo? It is an urban hidden gem that even most locals are unaware of.
    Excavated as a logistical lifeline during the Edo period, these canals offer a wondrous contrast between the historical scenery reminiscent of old Edo and the sprawling modern metropolis looming above.
    From the vast wetlands that once stretched across Tokyo Bay, to the grand city of Edo, and finally to one of the world's leading modern cities. You can witness another hidden side of Tokyo, a city that has continuously transformed like a living organism.

  13. [Bliss] Highly Fragrant, Freshly Hand-Roasted Coffee

    We provide a tea time featuring specialty coffee, roasted from green beans and drip-brewed right before your eyes. Enjoy the most luxurious cup of coffee—freshly roasted, freshly ground, and freshly brewed.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Q. Is it okay if I am a beginner and not very physically fit?
A. If you are participating alone, you will share a kayak paddled by your guide, so you can enjoy the tour with peace of mind even if you are not confident in your physical fitness.
Q. Will the kayak capsize?
A. Unlike the ocean or rapid rivers, the risk of capsizing is low. However, because the Sumida River has tidal currents and wakes from large ships, we are prepared for any unlikely event. To date, we have never had a capsizing incident.
