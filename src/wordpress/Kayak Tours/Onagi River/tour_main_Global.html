Rediscover Tokyo from a hidden perspective: a time-travel journey back to "Edo" (Old Tokyo) via its ancient waterways. Trace the historic logistics artery, the Onagi River, commissioned by the founding Shogun Tokugawa Ieyasu in the early 17th century. This 5km journey from East to West takes you through a dramatic landscape where the heritage of the Samurai era meets the silhouettes of modern skyscrapers. Experience the engineering marvel of "Mini Panama" lock gates twice as you transition between the Arakawa and Sumida Rivers. This calm, still-water tour is designed for everyone, including beginners and those not confident in their physical strength, offering a serene and cinematic "rediscovery" of Tokyo from just inches above the water.

★ Tour Highlights ★

  1. 【The Mini Panama Canal】A Massive Engineering Feat for Just One Kayak

    The Onagi River is a strategic canal opened in the early 17th century (1596–1615) along the coastline of Edo Bay (now Tokyo Bay) to connect Japan’s interior water networks. While its south side is now luxury high-rises built on reclaimed land, it was originally open sea. Due to geological subsidence from late 19th-century industrialization, this area sank significantly, becoming a "Zero-Meter Zone"—lying below sea level even at high tide. Parts of Eastern Koto-ku remain below sea level even during low tide.
    To protect the city from flooding, the canal's water level is artificially maintained below sea level. This creates a disconnect with the surrounding Sumida and Arakawa Rivers, which are linked to the tides. To solve this, the Oginobashi Lock (built 1976) and Arakawa Rock Gate (2005) were established. Operating exactly like the Panama Canal, these "ship elevators" handle water level differences of up to 3 meters. Whether it’s a large sightseeing boat or a single solo kayak, these massive gates will operate exclusively to guide you across this dramatic threshold.

  2. 【Edo’s Canals】The World-Class Water Heritage Unknown even to Locals

    Tokyo was once a "City of Water" comparable to Venice. While most modern residents are unaware of the intricate canal mesh running through districts like Koto and Chuo, these were once the lifelines of the shogunate capital, Edo. Before automobiles, wind and human power drove Japan’s logistics.
    The Onagi River was the vital artery connecting far-off northern Japan (Tohoku) to the castle town of Nihonbashi. Goods traveled a massive relay: from the Pacific to the Tone River, then through a series of canals, undergoing rigorous inspection at the Nakagawa Ship Guardhouse, crossing the Arakawa, and finally arriving at Nihonbashi—the historic "Kilometer Zero" of Japan. On this tour, you will physically retrace this heritage route.

  4. 【Perspective】Tracing the "Under-City"scape from Zero Meters

    By paddling on water levels lower than the surrounding city, you enter a sphere of absolute silence, isolated from the urban noise. From this unique "Low Angle" perspective, you can look up at the soaring skyscrapers of modern Tokyo while floating alongside the 400-year-old stone embankments of the samurai era. It is a three-dimensional experience of the city’s past and present.
